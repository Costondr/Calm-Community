It's not just physical.

It's the kind of tired that sleep doesn't fix. The kind that builds up across years of decisions, alerts, recalculations, and the constant, low-grade hum of a disease that never clocks out.

Diabetes burnout is real. And it's wildly underdiagnosed — not because doctors don't know about it, but because so many of us have gotten very good at pushing through it.

## What It Actually Feels Like

It's not always dramatic. It doesn't always look like skipping doses or ignoring your CGM for weeks.

Sometimes it looks like:

- Checking your blood sugar and feeling nothing. Not relief, not concern — just *nothing*.
- Eating something you know will spike you because you just can't do the math right now.
- Feeling angry at your own body for demanding this much attention.
- Smiling when someone says "at least it's manageable" — because explaining the truth feels like more work than you have left in you.

## You Are Not Failing

Here's what I want you to hear, and I mean it deeply:

The exhaustion you feel is not a character flaw. It is not weakness. It is the reasonable, predictable result of carrying an invisible weight that most people around you cannot see or fully understand.

You are not failing your disease. You are a human being navigating something genuinely hard.

## What Helps (And What Doesn't)

What doesn't help: being told to "stay positive," tracking more metrics, or pushing harder.

What sometimes helps:
- Naming it. Saying out loud — even just to yourself — "I am burnt out."
- Lowering the bar temporarily. Perfect management isn't the goal right now. Good enough is the goal.
- Finding your people. The ones who get it without explanation.

That last one is part of why CALM exists.

## You Don't Have to Earn Rest

You don't have to hit rock bottom to deserve support. You don't have to be in crisis to say "I'm struggling."

If you're tired — really, deeply tired — that's enough. You're allowed to rest. You're allowed to ask for help. You're allowed to be human.

We see you. We're here.
